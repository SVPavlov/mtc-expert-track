Section 01

The Existential Threat

80% of MTC's volume comes from the lease sector — which is targeting 90% electric fleets within 2 years. Their legacy fuel card business is evaporating. Competitors (MSPs, lease companies) are building their own charging/mobility products.

Meanwhile, a multi-year migration from a legacy platform to a modern stack is consuming the strategic capacity they need to innovate.

Section 03

Way of Working

The organization uses Scrum, but maturity varies dramatically across teams. Core Agile practices are inconsistent, and the foundations most teams rely on are either missing or not shared.

Scrum Adoption
  • ■ No shared Definition of Done — each team defines "done" differently, quality is negotiable under pressure
  • ■ No shared vocabulary — "initiative", "epic", "feature", "story" mean different things to different people
  • ■ Sprint Reviews are inconsistent — some teams do demos, some skip entirely, stakeholders rarely attend
  • ■ Refinement practices differ per domain — no standard for when or how PBIs get refined
  • ■ Scrum Master coverage has gaps — some teams operate without one
Product Management
  • ■ No shared Product Goal — 6 POs optimize for their own domains, not the whole product
  • ■ PM-PO collaboration model is undefined — reporting line changed, no new operating model designed
  • ■ No cross-domain coordination — no mechanism for POs to align priorities across domains
  • ■ OKRs introduced but disconnected from Sprint-level work — teams don't see the link
  • ■ Stakeholder management is ad-hoc — no clear interface for who talks to whom

Section 05

People Dynamics

12 individual intakes completed. Patterns distilled below — no individual profiles.

Pattern

Ownership Instability

4 of 6 POs are new or reshuffled in the last 4 months. Only one has been in role longer than a year. One stepped down — the role was overwhelming without proper support. High churn erodes team trust and product continuity.

Critical 6 POs affected
Pattern

Capacity Overload

Multiple POs report being stretched beyond capacity — constant meetings, operational firefighting, dual roles. Some lack Scrum Master support. Burnout risk flagged across multiple intake conversations. Several participants anticipate a breaking point within weeks.

Burnout Risk Systemic
Pattern

Skill Gaps

Several POs entered the role from technical or business backgrounds without formal product ownership training. Task-focused rather than value-focused. Strategic thinking and stakeholder management are underdeveloped across the group.

Development Needed Multiple POs
Pattern

Structural Isolation

Product management is understaffed — one PM for the entire product. PO reporting recently moved to a different C-level. Cross-domain knowledge is concentrated in a few individuals. Losing any one of them creates a critical gap.

Single Points of Failure
Pattern

Hidden Strengths

Cross-cutting knowledge exists in support roles (analysis, architecture) but isn't connected to product decision-making. Some experienced participants are pragmatic and self-aware — they want practical improvements, not more theory. Energy for change exists.

Opportunity Leverage Point
Pattern

Vocabulary Fragmentation

No shared definitions for basic constructs (initiative, epic, feature, story). Different people use the same words to mean different things. This creates friction in every planning conversation and makes cross-team coordination nearly impossible.

Foundation Missing

Session 02 — March 11, 2026

What We Learned: PM-PO Collaboration

Agreements Don't Stick
  • ■ Agreements are made, votes taken — then "everyone goes in a different direction"
  • ■ Decisions reversed when absent stakeholders disagree
  • ■ Way of Working treated as "a flavor you can put in your coffee or not"
  • ■ "We had more exceptions than we were working" — process hollowed out by claimed exemptions
  • ■ No meeting notes — new PO hasn't received any in a month
Seeds Without Crops
  • ■ "We plant seeds but don't wait for crops to grow"
  • ■ No consequences when people deviate from agreements
  • ■ Platform health deprioritized despite investment — "everyone else is busy with their own thing"
  • ■ Office manager expects "this will change again" — cynicism visible even outside IT
  • ■ Proven in real time: one canvas group had a great discussion, then wrote nothing down
Responsibility Hot Potato
  • ■ "That's the responsibility of the person below me" — delegated down, never followed up
  • ■ Escalation = "involving papa or mama" to get your way, not shared ownership
  • ■ PO committee averages decisions — optimized for nobody
  • ■ "We are just a democracy" — but decisions still require someone who decides
Meeting Culture Problem

"We have stacked up opinions, acknowledge them, don't combine them, do nothing with them. Just words in a room. Ventilated."

"I don't mind listening to one person. I'm more confused by hearing all opinions all the time with no outcome."

  • ■ Side conversations build frustration in private instead of being expressed openly
  • ■ People mentally check out, then have sidebar discussions that create noise and division
No Roadmap, No Connection
  • ■ No product roadmap exists — "We have OKRs" but OKRs are disconnected from delivery
  • ■ OKRs + SLDC projects + platform issues all compete for PO attention simultaneously
  • ■ Team-level roadmaps attempted — "that's not a product roadmap"
  • ■ "When are we successful this quarter?" — "We won't be successful"
  • ■ Output vs outcome breakthrough: delivering a pricing feature ≠ driving EV acquisition increase
Anti-Patterns Recognized
  • ■ Product-part owners — POs own domains, not the product
  • ■ Fixes that Backfire — more backlogs → more dependencies → less value
  • ■ Zombie Scrum — "zombies walking to the TV screen at 9 AM"
  • ■ Output over outcome — teams focused on tickets, not value
  • ■ Agile as excuse — "We don't do predictable delivery. We are agile now."
  • ■ Custom feature toxicity — historical trauma from client-specific builds. "Horrible. Sometimes."
  • ■ Developers as order-takers — "I'm trying to convince them they are people brains, but they just want to be told what to do"
Coordination Emerging
  • ■ Groups designed two-level coordination — macro (PO alignment) + micro (team delivery)
  • ■ Dependency types recognized: sequential, reciprocal, pooled resource
  • ■ Agreement: don't plan to full capacity — need slack for unknowns
  • ■ Product-part owner pattern recognized — "that's exactly what we have"
  • ■ "The Dragon Zone" — participant-coined term for daring each other to make bolder decisions

Key Insight from Module 2

The group can diagnose its own dysfunction with remarkable accuracy. Every anti-pattern was self-identified, not taught. The challenge is no longer awareness — it's follow-through. Module 3 must bridge the gap between "we know what's wrong" and "we're actually changing it."

Between Sessions — Due March 24, 2026

Prepare for Module 3: OKRs That Work

Two weeks between sessions. These assignments are designed to be done during your normal work — no extra meetings, no extra documents. Just pay attention differently.

Assignment 1: The Agreement Test
  • 1. Pick one agreement from the last PO alignment or team meeting
  • 2. Write it down in one sentence (if you can't, it wasn't clear)
  • 3. After 1 week, check: is it still being followed?
  • 4. Bring your finding to Module 3 — what happened and why?
Time needed
5 min to write it down + 5 min to check after 1 week
Assignment 2: OKR Reality Check
  • 1. Find your team's current OKRs (Miro board, spreadsheet, wherever they live)
  • 2. For each Key Result, answer: can you measure progress right now?
  • 3. Ask one team member: "What are our OKRs?" — compare their answer to reality
  • 4. Note: is there a connection between your OKRs and your Sprint Goals?
Time needed
15 min to review OKRs + 5 min conversation with team member
Assignment 3: Sprint Review Experiment
  • ☐ Let a developer present instead of you
  • ☐ Start with the Product Goal, not the Sprint output
  • ☐ End with: "What should change on the Product Backlog?"
  • ☐ Invite one stakeholder who's never attended
  • ☐ Cancel one other meeting and fold it into the review
Report back
What did you try? What happened? Would you do it again?
Stretch Goal: Cross-Domain Coffee
  • ▶ Have a 15-minute coffee with a PO from a different domain
  • ▶ Ask them: "What's the biggest dependency between our domains right now?"
  • ▶ Share what you learned in Module 3 — we'll use it for the OKR alignment exercise
Why this matters
OKRs only work when dependencies are visible. This conversation makes one dependency concrete before we start designing OKR alignment.

Peer Review at Module 3

Module 3 starts with a 15-minute peer exchange. Pair up with someone from a different domain. Share your findings from all three assignments. Challenge each other: "Did the agreement stick? Can you measure your Key Results? What did your Sprint Review experiment teach you?"

No slides. No reports. Just bring your observations and be honest.
